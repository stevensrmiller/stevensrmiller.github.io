Seamless Repetition

Suppose we have a cool pattern we'd like to use repeatedly, to cover a wall or the sides of an object. Maybe this one:

 

If we simply duplicate it and put the duplicate beside the original, there is an obvious seam in the middle where the original and the duplicate touch each other:

 

Notice, however, that because each half of the picture above is the same, this means that the middle of each half is exactly the same as the middle of the other half. We can use this fact to make a new picture out of the center part of the one above. This new one will be the same size as the original pattern we started with, but its left edge will be a match for its right edge. Here's the part we'll use:

 

Once we've cut that part out, it looks like this:

 

If we duplicate it and put the duplicate copy beside the original, the seam in the middle is gone, because the left edge of the duplicate is a match for the right edge of the original:

 

But that's not much better since there are now two seams, even though there isn't a seam where the original and copy join together.

To solve this problem, we will "fade" out the central region of that image we just cut out of the middle of the original and its duplicate. We will do this by using a "layer mask," which sets an amount of transparency for each point in the image. It's like the image is painted onto a sheet of glass. At the edges, the paint (that is, the image) will be completely opaque. As we move from the edges towards the center, we'll make the paint (again, that's the image) more and more transparent until, in the exact center, we'll just have clear glass:

 

 

The seam in the middle isn't visible anymore, because the picture is completely transparent there. In the image above, it's as though you are looking through the transparent center at the white background behind it. Because the left and right edge of the picture still match, we could, again, duplicate it and put the duplicate copy beside the one above:

 

Now there is again no seam in the middle, and the seams that were in the middles of each half are invisible because they are transparent. But this isn't much better, since those transparent sections  just look like big white, hazy vertical strips. To solve this problem, we will go back to our previous image with the transparent center:

 

But now, instead of looking through the transparent center at the white background, we will put a copy of the very first picture we used behand the one above, so we will see the center of that when we look through the transparent center of the image above. Here's the result:

 

One way to understand what you are seeing above is to think of the original picture having its own layer mask, but one that is doing the reverse of the layer mask we used to make the middle transparent. For our original, we are doing the equivalent of using a layer mask that makes it opaque in the middle and transparent at the edges:

 

The end result is that the combined picture isn't transparent anywhere. At the edges, you are seeing only the copy with the seam in the middle, and none of the original. At the center, you are seeing only the original and none of the copy. Everywhere else, you are seeing a blend of the copy and of the original. If we take that combined picture and duplicate it, then put the duplicate copy beside the original combined picture, there is no seam anywhere:

 

We can do this as many times as we like, copying and repeating our combined image, and there is never a visible seam:

 

The image is repeating, but there are no seams anywhere.
